Magnesium Citrate vs Glycinate: Choosing the Right Form

Last updated: February 2026 · 10 min read

Magnesium is one of the most important minerals for brain and body function—involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions. But walk into any supplement aisle and you'll face a bewildering array of forms: citrate, glycinate, oxide, threonate, taurate, and more.

Two of the most popular and well-absorbed forms are magnesium citrate and magnesium glycinate (bisglycinate). They're often recommended interchangeably, but they have meaningful differences in how they work, what they're best for, and how your body responds. Here's what the research shows.

Key Takeaways

  • Both are well-absorbed: Citrate and glycinate have significantly better bioavailability than cheap forms like magnesium oxide.
  • Different strengths: Citrate is better for general magnesium repletion and digestive regularity. Glycinate is preferred for sleep, relaxation, and anxiety support.
  • Digestive tolerance differs: Citrate can cause loose stools at higher doses. Glycinate is gentler on the stomach.
  • The glycine in glycinate adds value: Glycine itself is a calming amino acid that supports sleep quality independently of magnesium.
  • Your ideal form depends on your goals: There's no universally "better" form—it depends on what you're trying to achieve.

Understanding Magnesium Bioavailability

Not all magnesium supplements deliver the same amount of usable magnesium to your body. Bioavailability—the percentage that actually gets absorbed into your bloodstream—varies dramatically by form. This is the most important factor most people overlook when choosing a magnesium supplement.

Inorganic forms like magnesium oxide contain high amounts of elemental magnesium per capsule (about 60% by weight) but have poor absorption rates—some studies estimate as low as 4%. Organic forms like citrate and glycinate contain less elemental magnesium per capsule but are absorbed far more efficiently.

What the Research Shows

Study: Predicting and Testing Bioavailability of Magnesium Supplements (2019)

Blancquaert et al. reviewed the bioavailability of different magnesium salt forms, examining both solubility and actual absorption in human subjects.

Key Findings: Organic magnesium salts (including citrate and glycinate) consistently demonstrated superior bioavailability compared to inorganic salts like magnesium oxide. Magnesium citrate showed high solubility, which correlated with better absorption. Magnesium bisglycinate may benefit from an additional absorption pathway—the dipeptide transporter—which could enhance uptake beyond standard mineral absorption.

Source: Blancquaert et al., Nutrients, 2019 (PMC ID: PMC6683096)

Study: Magnesium Bisglycinate Supplementation in Healthy Adults Reporting Poor Sleep: A Randomized, Placebo-Controlled Trial (2025)

This randomized, placebo-controlled trial evaluated the effects of magnesium bisglycinate supplementation (providing 250mg elemental magnesium and 1,523mg glycine daily) on sleep quality in adults with self-reported poor sleep.

Key Findings: The magnesium bisglycinate group showed a statistically significant reduction in Insomnia Severity Index (ISI) scores compared to placebo. Most improvements occurred within the first 14 days and were sustained throughout the study period. The researchers noted that both the magnesium and glycine components likely contributed to sleep benefits.

Source: Published in PMC, 2025 (PMC ID: PMC12412596)

Study: Bioavailability of Magnesium Food Supplements: A Systematic Review (2021)

Uysal et al. systematically reviewed studies on magnesium supplement bioavailability, examining how different forms, doses, and conditions affect absorption.

Key Findings: The review confirmed that organic magnesium forms (citrate, glycinate, lactate) are better absorbed than inorganic forms. Absorption is dose-dependent—higher single doses result in proportionally lower absorption rates. Taking magnesium on an empty stomach may improve absorption. The chelation of magnesium with amino acids (as in glycinate) provides an alternative intestinal absorption route via dipeptide transporters.

Source: Uysal et al., Nutrition, 2021 (ScienceDirect)

Magnesium Citrate: Benefits and Best Uses

Magnesium citrate is magnesium bound to citric acid. It's one of the most commonly recommended forms due to its high solubility and proven absorption.

Strengths

  • High solubility: Dissolves readily in water, which correlates with better intestinal absorption.
  • Well-studied: Extensively used in clinical research on magnesium supplementation.
  • Affordable: Generally less expensive than chelated forms like glycinate.
  • Digestive benefits: The osmotic effect can help with occasional constipation.
  • Good for general repletion: If you're magnesium-deficient, citrate can efficiently raise your levels.

Limitations

  • Digestive side effects: The same osmotic effect that helps with constipation can cause loose stools or diarrhea, especially at doses above 300mg of elemental magnesium.
  • Less ideal for nighttime use: If digestive effects disrupt your sleep, citrate may not be the best bedtime supplement.
  • No additional amino acid benefits: Unlike glycinate, the citric acid component doesn't provide independent health benefits.

Magnesium Glycinate: Benefits and Best Uses

Magnesium glycinate (or bisglycinate) is magnesium chelated with the amino acid glycine. This combination provides both the benefits of magnesium and the calming properties of glycine.

Strengths

  • Excellent for sleep: Clinical trials demonstrate improvements in insomnia severity scores. The glycine component independently supports sleep quality by lowering core body temperature and modulating NMDA receptors.
  • Gentle on digestion: Significantly less likely to cause loose stools compared to citrate, making it suitable for people with sensitive stomachs.
  • Calming effect: Both magnesium and glycine support GABA activity and nervous system relaxation, making this form particularly useful for stress and anxiety support.
  • Dual absorption pathway: May be absorbed via both mineral and dipeptide transporter pathways.

Limitations

  • Higher cost: Chelated magnesium forms are typically more expensive to produce.
  • More capsules needed: Lower elemental magnesium per capsule means you may need to take more pills to reach your target dose.
  • Not ideal for constipation: If you specifically want magnesium's digestive motility benefits, glycinate won't provide them.

Choosing Based on Your Goals

For Sleep and Relaxation

Choose magnesium glycinate. The combination of magnesium's GABA-enhancing effects with glycine's calming properties makes this the optimal form for sleep support. Take 200-400mg of elemental magnesium (from glycinate) 1-2 hours before bed.

For General Magnesium Repletion

Choose magnesium citrate. If your primary goal is correcting a deficiency or maintaining adequate magnesium levels, citrate is effective, well-absorbed, and cost-efficient. Take with meals to minimize digestive effects.

For Digestive Regularity

Choose magnesium citrate. Its osmotic properties support healthy bowel function. If constipation is a concern, citrate serves double duty as both a magnesium supplement and gentle digestive aid.

For Cognitive Support

Consider magnesium L-threonate instead. While both citrate and glycinate support brain health through general magnesium repletion, L-threonate specifically crosses the blood-brain barrier and may be more effective for cognitive benefits.

Individual Variation: Why Your Experience May Differ

Two people taking the same magnesium form at the same dose can have completely different experiences. Here's why:

Baseline Magnesium Status

Standard blood tests (serum magnesium) only measure about 1% of your body's total magnesium and can appear normal even when tissue levels are depleted. Red blood cell (RBC) magnesium is a better marker but still imperfect. If you're genuinely depleted, you'll likely notice more dramatic effects from either form.

Gut Health and Absorption

Conditions affecting intestinal health—including inflammatory bowel disease, celiac disease, or even chronic stress—can impair magnesium absorption. Your gut microbiome composition also influences mineral uptake. This means two people taking the same supplement may absorb very different amounts.

Diet and Cofactors

Magnesium absorption is enhanced by vitamin D and inhibited by high doses of calcium, zinc, or phytates (found in grains and legumes). Your overall diet context matters as much as the supplement itself.

Medication Interactions

Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), certain antibiotics, and diuretics can all affect magnesium levels and absorption. If you take any of these, your magnesium needs and response may differ from baseline recommendations.

How to Track Your Response

Given how many variables affect magnesium's impact, personal tracking is the most reliable way to determine which form works best for you:

  1. Pick one form to start: Based on your primary goal (sleep, general health, digestion), choose either citrate or glycinate.
  2. Establish baseline metrics: For at least one week, track sleep quality, energy levels, afternoon mental clarity, mood, and any digestive symptoms.
  3. Supplement consistently for 3-4 weeks: Take the same dose at the same time each day.
  4. Track daily: Note the same metrics you tracked at baseline. Include any side effects.
  5. Evaluate and adjust: Compare your supplementation period to your baseline. If you want to compare forms, take a one-week washout period before switching.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which is better absorbed, magnesium citrate or glycinate?

Both forms have high bioavailability compared to inorganic forms like magnesium oxide. Research suggests they are absorbed at similar rates. Magnesium citrate relies on standard mineral absorption pathways enhanced by high solubility, while magnesium glycinate may also be absorbed via the dipeptide transporter pathway, potentially offering an additional absorption route.

Is magnesium glycinate better for sleep than citrate?

Generally, yes. The glycine component of magnesium glycinate has its own calming and sleep-promoting properties. A 2025 randomized controlled trial found that magnesium bisglycinate supplementation significantly reduced insomnia severity within 14 days. Citrate can also support sleep through magnesium repletion, but it may cause digestive effects that disrupt sleep for some people.

Does magnesium citrate cause diarrhea?

Magnesium citrate has an osmotic effect in the gut, drawing water into the intestines. At higher doses (generally above 300mg of elemental magnesium), this can cause loose stools or diarrhea. This property makes it useful for constipation relief but less ideal for people with sensitive digestion. Splitting your dose across the day can help minimize this effect.

Can I take magnesium citrate and glycinate together?

Yes. Some people take citrate in the morning for energy and digestive support, and glycinate in the evening for relaxation and sleep. Keep your total elemental magnesium from supplements within 200-400mg daily unless directed otherwise by a healthcare provider.

How much magnesium should I take daily?

The RDA for magnesium is 310-420mg daily for adults, depending on age and sex. Since you get some magnesium from food, supplemental doses of 200-400mg are common. Start at the lower end and adjust based on your response and any digestive effects. More is not always better—excess magnesium is excreted by the kidneys in healthy individuals.
